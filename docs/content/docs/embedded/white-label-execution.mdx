White-label execution is the core promise of the [Embedded iPaaS](/embedded): your customers get a fully featured workflow engine and an integration builder inside your product, with your branding, your URL, your user experience.

## The mental model

```
       Your SaaS product
       ┌────────────────────────────────┐
       │  Your UI / Your branding       │
       │  ┌──────────────────────────┐  │
       │  │  ByteChef-rendered       │  │
       │  │  workflow builder        │  │  ◄── embedded via iframe / SDK
       │  │  (white-labeled)         │  │
       │  └──────────────────────────┘  │
       │  ┌──────────────────────────┐  │
       │  │  Your business logic     │  │
       │  └──────────────────────────┘  │
       └────────────────────────────────┘
                      │
                      ▼
       ┌────────────────────────────────┐
       │  ByteChef Enterprise           │
       │  ┌──────────────┐  ┌────────┐  │
       │  │ Tenant A     │  │ Tenant │  │
       │  │ workspace    │  │   B    │  │  ◄── per-customer isolation
       │  └──────────────┘  └────────┘  │
       └────────────────────────────────┘
```

Your customer never sees "ByteChef." They see your product offering integrations.

## What "white-label" actually covers

| Surface | White-labeled? |
|---|---|
| **UI shell** | Your product chrome wraps the workflow builder. |
| **Workflow builder** | Renders inside your product (iframe or SDK component) with your CSS theme tokens. |
| **Component catalog** | The 200+ built-in components are visible to your customers; the "ByteChef" branding is removed. |
| **Customer-facing emails** | Sent from your domain, with your templates. |
| **Webhook URLs** | Live under your domain. |
| **Documentation links** | Point at your help center, not ByteChef's. |
| **Error messages** | Translated to your voice and locale. |

## How customers interact with it

A typical embedded flow:

1. **Customer signs in** to your product as they always do.
2. **Customer navigates to "Integrations"** (or whatever you call it).
3. **They see a list of integration templates** you've defined for your product — pre-built workflows that solve common scenarios.
4. **They configure a connection** to their external system (Salesforce, etc.) by completing the standard auth flow. The credentials live in [encrypted credential storage](/enterprise/governance-security/encrypted-credentials), scoped to their tenant.
5. **The workflow runs** every time the trigger fires. Execution history is visible to them; nothing leaves your product.

## Two integration models

### Pre-built templates

You define a library of integrations as templates. Customers don't build from scratch — they pick a template, configure their connections and parameters, and turn it on. Best for product teams that want curated, supported integrations.

### Builder access

You give customers the visual workflow builder and let them build whatever they want. Best for technical customers and developer-tool products.

You can mix — offer templates as the on-ramp, expose the builder for power users.

## What "execution" means here

When a customer's workflow runs:

- It runs on **your** ByteChef cluster, isolated to their tenant.
- It uses **their** connections (their Salesforce key, their Slack token).
- It executes against **their** business logic (your custom components plus the built-in ones).
- It generates execution history they can see in your product UI.

Crucially, your other customers never share runtime — see [tenant-isolated security](/embedded/tenant-isolated-security).

## Custom components for your product's logic

The integration catalog you give customers can include [custom components](/enterprise/extensibility/custom-components) you write — for example, a "Your Product" component with actions like "create a record" or "look up a customer." That lets you bundle your own product as a first-class integration point in the workflows your customers build.

## What you don't have to do

- **Build a visual workflow builder.**
- **Build connectors.**
- **Build an execution engine.**
- **Build credential storage.**
- **Build a webhook ingress.**
- **Build a scheduler.**
- **Build observability and execution history.**

All of those come with the platform. You build **the product wrapper** — branding, billing, customer onboarding, your business logic.

## The promise to your customers

Embedded gives your customers:

- **Hundreds of integrations** without you having to build them.
- **A visual workflow builder** they can use without writing code.
- **Their own connections** — they bring their own credentials; you don't see them.
- **Their data, isolated** — your other customers can't see their workflows or data.

## The promise to you

- **No integration team.** ByteChef ships and maintains the [200+ built-in components](/enterprise/extensibility/built-in-components).
- **No new infrastructure to operate** — Embedded is the same binary you've already deployed.
- **One billing surface.** You charge your customers; ByteChef bills you.
- **Source-available engine.** No vendor lock-in on the part of your product that powers a critical workflow surface.
